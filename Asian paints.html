Why is the P/E in this zone?
Asian Paints' current P/E of 65.2x sits modestly below its 5-year average of 70.1x, placing it in the FAIR VALUE zone relative to its own history. However, this is misleading: the 5-year average is inflated by the FY22 P/E of 97.5x (when earnings were temporarily depressed post-COVID). The stock is expensive on absolute terms (87% premium to sector) and on PEG (4.85x). The elevated multiple reflects the market's expectation that Asian Paints' dominant brand and distribution network will enable it to weather the current competitive storm — a thesis that remains unproven. Earnings have declined for 3 of the last 4 quarters YoY, and the FY25 profit drop of 33% suggests the P/E may be a value trap: the 'E' is falling faster than the 'P'.
Overall valuation: MIXED — EXPENSIVE vs sector; FAIR vs own history
Asian Paints trades at a significant premium to peers (P/E 65x vs 35x sector) and offers a lower earnings yield (1.53%) than risk-free FD rates (~7%). The current P/E is near its 5-year average, but the average itself is elevated by historical standards. For a 3-year investor, the key question is whether earnings can compound fast enough to justify the multiple — current growth rates (5% revenue CAGR, 8% profit CAGR) do not support the valuation.

Ownership Analysis (Investment Perspective)
Promoters: Holding steady at 52.63% for over 12 quarters — a strong signal of promoter confidence. No dilution or stake sale despite the stock's underperformance. Pledge at 8.60% is within acceptable limits.

FIIs: Have been net sellers over 3 years (from ~17% to 12.1%), but the Dec 2025 quarter showed a notable uptick from 11.64% to 12.78%, suggesting some foreign investors see value at lower levels. The Mar 2026 quarter saw a slight dip to 12.11%.

DIIs: The big story — DII holding has surged from 15.58% to 21.81% in one year. Domestic mutual funds and institutions are aggressively buying what FIIs are selling, indicating strong domestic conviction in the long-term story.

Overall: The ownership shift from FIIs to DIIs provides stability (DII money is typically stickier) but also means the stock may be more vulnerable during domestic liquidity crunches. Combined institutional holding (FII + DII) at ~34% provides good governance oversight and liquidity.

SWOT Summary — Investment Perspective

📖 Definitions — tap to expand

P/E ratio — What you pay per ₹1 of profit. Lower vs history and sector = cheaper.

PEG Ratio — P/E divided by earnings growth rate. Below 1 = growth available at a reasonable price.

P/B ratio — Price vs net assets. Below 1 = buying at a discount to book value.

P/Sales — Price vs revenue. Useful when profits are cyclical or recovering.

EV/EBITDA — Full business value check including debt. Lower = better.

Earnings Yield — EPS / CMP. Compare to risk-free rate (~7%).

ROE — Profit generated per ₹100 of shareholder equity. Above 15% = good.

ROCE — How efficiently total capital is used. Above 15% = healthy.

ROA — Profit as % of total assets.

Free Cash Flow — Cash left after all expenses and investments.

Debtor Days — Average time to collect payment. Below 30 days = excellent.

Piotroski Score — 0–9 score measuring financial health. 7–9 = strong.

RSI — Relative Strength Index. Below 30 = oversold, above 70 = overbought.

MACD — Moving Average Convergence Divergence. Bullish crossover = positive signal.

ADX — Average Directional Index. Above 25 = strong trend.

Promoter pledging — Founders borrowing against their shares. Above 10% = red flag.

CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate.
